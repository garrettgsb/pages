Disclaimer

This page seems as convenient a place as any to hold my thoughts on how to develop tools that can be used to further the pursuit of a human-like AGI (Artificial General Intelligence).

The following can be thought of as a brainstorm, or even as a mood board, moreso than as any sort of proposal for action. I make no claim that the ideas herein are actionable, reasonable, or even sensible, and I leave it to the reader to draw their own conclusions as to the best approach to interacting with robot brains.

Here is mine.

My Approach

The task is to develop tools that will be used by a group of people much smarter than me, to tinker with something that is much smarter than them. My gut reaction is to stick to what I know; to lean on my area of greatest expertise... But unfortunately, I don't have one of those, so a different approach is needed:

My first source of inspiration comes from biology. If a digital intelligence is meant to have characteristics similar to those of a human, then perhaps a sensible place to start is to examine how we learn about and tinker with humans.

We should wrap the AI in a thermometric chainmail suit, is what I'm suggesting.

Despite dealing with a system that is not particularly easy to debug, physicians and medical researchers have developed a diverse bag of hacks toolkit of techniques to peer into the inner workings of the human body, and sometimes to make modifications to its normal functioning.

However, while the typical tools of technology and industry can be used to melt, bend, grind, or otherwise coerce materials to match the vision of engineers, the tools of medicine can't force an organism to behave in one way or another, and trying too hard to do so will often simply upset and destroy the delicate system.

Rather, medical tools must apply only the slightest, most tactful nudge. It's no coincidence, then, that we often describe any sort of extremely delicate operation as "surgically precise."

Perhaps the tools used to interact with a digital intelligence ought to be created with the same attentiveness and humility.

Inspiration From Medicine

The human body is doing exactly 10,000 things simultaneously[citation needed], and observing these processes can give us clues about the state of the system.

A common theme in medical diagnostics, then, is in choosing a specific thing to measure, and then amplifying that signal, thereby bringing it to the forefront of the examiner's attention. Sometimes, we can do that fairly directly, like when we take someone's pulse... Other times, the signal is sufficiently elusive that we need a clever way amplify and measure it. In those cases, we might choose to do something like inject a patient with radioactive material, and then measure where the gamma rays shoot out.

For real. Let's talk about that.

Radiopharmaceuticals and SPECT scans

Or: Injecting a patient with radioactive material and then meaturing where the gamma rays shoot out

A SPECT scan is a versatile tool for monitoring biological activity. Normally, things found in the human body do not emit gamma rays, and for good reason, but if one of those things did, it would be very convenient to observe how that particular thing behaves in the body.

That's great news, because we can actually attach something radioactive to nearly anything that we might be interested in following (like a sugar molecule, a particular hormone, or even a living cell), and then set it free in the body.

This is called "radiolabeling," and it's a great way to debug things like internal bleeding (because you can inject it), or lung dysfunction (because you can snort it). The radiolabeled thing goes on to do what it was going to do anyway, but now, because it's loudly spraying gamma rays all over the place, we can see it clearly with a SPECT scanner.

A SPECT scan measuring blood flow

Then, we can compare what we see with our expectation of what should happen according to our hypothesis, and determine whether:

  1. Everything is okay
  2. Something is wrong
  3. We've learned something new
This pattern of passing a "loud" carrier signal through the system and then observing its effect, is instructive as a methodology for interacting with complex, autonomous systems, and at the very least, offers inspiration as to how this information might be visualized

Electricity: Try It On Your Friends

A favorite technique of human performance researchers and mad scientists, gentle amounts of electricity applied to human tissue can tell us neat things about how the body functions. Electromyography, for instance, is used for measuring whether or not nerves are doing what we think they are.

More on this later. Eventually I want to talk about probing brains with electrodes, but I don't really know where I'm going with this at the moment.

Why those suck

Okay, they don't suck. Sorry for the clickbait sub-title. But they aren't nearly powerful enough for our purposes. I don't know for sure, but my intuition is that the current tools used for working with neural nets fall short when they're being used by a general intelligence. The interactive visualizations are neat, but so far it seems as though they are just used for demonstration purposes, and aren't nearly powerful enough to be used in a real life project.

They give us some clues, however, as to some of the characteristics that good tools used to interact with a human-like AGI might have:

  • You can filter stuff
  • You can change parameters directly
  • You can play back stuff that has happened
  • You can do some fourth thing

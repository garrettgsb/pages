Disclaimer

This page seems as convenient a place as any to hold my thoughts on how to develop tools that can be used to further the pursuit of a human-like AGI (Artificial General Intelligence). It is far from finished, and will remain a work in progress for some time.

The following can be thought of as a brainstorm, or even as a mood board, moreso than as any sort of proposal for action. I make no claim that the ideas herein are actionable, reasonable, or good, and I leave it to the reader to make their own conjecture as to the best approach to interacting with robot brains.

Here is mine.

My Approach

The task is to develop tools that will be used by a group of people much smarter than me, to tinker with something that is much smarter than them. My gut reaction is to stick to what I know; to lean on my area of greatest expertise... But unfortunately, I don't have one of those, so a different approach is needed:

My first source of inspiration comes from biology. If a digital intelligence is meant to have characteristics similar to those of a human, then perhaps a sensible place to start is to examine how we learn about and tinker with humans.

We should wrap the AI in a thermometric chainmail suit, is what I'm suggesting.

Despite dealing with a system that is not particularly easy to debug, physicians and medical researchers have developed a diverse bag of hacks toolkit of techniques to peer into the inner workings of the human body, and sometimes to make modifications to its normal functioning.

However, while the typical tools of technology and industry can be used to melt, bend, grind, or otherwise coerce materials to match the vision of engineers, the tools of medicine can't force an organism to behave in one way or another, and trying too hard to do so will often simply upset and destroy the delicate system.

Rather, medical tools must apply only the slightest, most tactful nudge. It's no coincidence that other disciplines, from military to manufacturing, borrow terminology from medicine when they describe any sort of extremely delicate operation as "surgically precise."

Perhaps the tools used to interact with a complex digital intelligence ought to be created with the same attentiveness and humility.

Inspiration From Medicine

The human body is doing exactly 10,000 things[citation needed] at any given time, and observing these processes can give us clues about the state of the system.

A common theme in medical diagnostics, then, is in choosing a specific thing to measure, and then amplifying that signal, thereby bringing it to the forefront of the examiner's attention. Sometimes, we can do that fairly directly, like when we take someone's pulse; Other times, we're interested in a more elusive signal. In that case, we need a clever way to coax it into a form that is convenient to measure, so we might feel compelled to do something like, say, inject a patient with radioactive material so we can measure where the gamma rays shoot out.

For real. Let's talk about that.

Radiopharmaceuticals and SPECT scans

Or: Injecting a patient with radioactive material so we can measure where the gamma rays shoot out

A SPECT scan is a versatile tool for monitoring biological activity. Normally, things found in the human body do not emit gamma rays, and for good reason, but if one of those things did, it would be very convenient to observe how that particular thing behaves in the body.

That's great news, because we can actually attach radioactive particles to nearly anything that we might be interested in following (like a sugar molecule, a particular hormone, or even a living cell), and then set it free in the body.

This is called "radiolabeling," and it's a great way to debug things like internal bleeding (because you can inject it), or lung dysfunction (because you can snort it).

A SPECT scan measuring blood flow

The radiolabeled thing goes on to do what it was going to do anyway, but now it's loudly spraying gamma rays all over the place, so we can see it clearly with a SPECT scanner. Then, we can compare what we see with our expectation of what should happen according to our hypothesis, and determine whether:

  1. Everything is okay
  2. Something is wrong
  3. We've learned something new
This pattern of passing a "loud" carrier signal through the system and then observing its behavior, is instructive as a tactic for interacting with any complex, autonomous system. I see the tools used to interact with a sophisticated AGI as a thin layer of visibility on top of a tremendously deep (but mostly opaque) system, much like the tools of medical diagnosis.

Visualizing

One of the most profound lessons in human perception that I know of came from the alt text of an XKCD comic that has nothing to do with data visualization: 

            "Our brains have just one scale, and we resize our experiences to fit."

We are best equipped to intuitively understand "medium-sized objects moving at medium speeds through medium distances," so data presentation is largely about finding ways to represent things in medium magnitudes. I like to think of this as "zoom level."

To create a meaningful visualization of something, I think it's helpful to look at how others have represented things with similar characteristics, and then use that to inspire an approach that suits our needs. The first place I thought to look was at neural nets.

How people have been visualizing neural networks

Neural networks seem like a good place to start thinking about how to represent what's going on inside of a human-like robot brain. I still don't know a whole lot about either of those things, but there is a wealth of literature on the tools and techniques used to make them do neat tricks.

The question is, can those techniques be elaborated into more powerful, sophisticated tools?

Probably! Of course, I don't know yet what specific problems we want to solve, but here are some observations and conjectures.

Deep learning seems to likes conventional graphs

It seems as though right now, people working with neural networks are still very reliant on 2D conventional graphs. I like graphs alot, but we know that they are not a panacea for all of our data woes.

Interactive visualizations of neural networks

Simulations like the TensorFlow Playground are great for playing with the concept of neural nets, and the way that they lay out controls and data may be hints as to how a more sophisticated toolset could look.

I think it's particularly intuitive how this UI puts its controls in "reading order," and the way they use color is effective: Only things that represent data get any coloration; configuration and controls are otherwise all in grayscale.

Ryerson University's visuslization of a convoluted neural network is particularly interesting, because it addresses one of my initial thoughts about representing data in 3D: 

Is 3D EVER a good idea for a visualization?

While I still think that most of the time, making a visualization 3D adds distracting complexity, I'm convinced now that a full 3D representation could actually be meaningful in this sort of application. Good work, Ryerson.

Those aren't good enough, though

These tools don't seem nearly robust enough for complex systems with overwhelming amounts of data. I don't know for sure, but my intuition is that the current tools used for working with neural nets fall short when they're being applied to a general intelligence.

The interactive visualizations are neat, but shallow. They are useful for demos, but they don't give any particularly powerful capabilities. I feel as though these particular layouts would quickly become cluttered and unusable if they were implemented on a large scale.

They give us some clues, however, as to some of the characteristics that good tools used to interact with a human-like AGI might have:

  • Zooming in and out: View the whole system, or focus on one tiny part
  • Filtering out information in which we are not interested
  • Making both tiny, targeted adjustments, and system-wide configuration changes
  • Playing back past system states
  • Some fifth thing (I swear I had one...)

These insights have given me a few ideas as to how these desirable functions can be interpereted as features. Here are some rough notes on the shapes they could take.

Fun visualizations that aren't related to AI (yet)

Force-directed graphs

Below is a force-directed graph that represents all of the connections between the characters in "Les MisÃ©rables." Every time the characters share a scene in the script, they are said to have "encountered" one another, and a link is formed. If they encounter each other again, that link's "value" is increased by one, and the link is made stronger.

I like force-directed graphs because every property can carry meaning. The software knows what to do without too much "massaging" of the data by the user:

  • The color of the nodes can represent qualitative data, like groupings or categories
  • The size of the nodes could represent any arbitrary quantitative property of the node itself
    • The graph below doesn't do this, but in the context of "Les MisÃ©rables," perhaps the size of the node could represent the number of lines that a character speaks, which might indicate how important that character is.
  • The length (and 'springiness!') of the edges (lines) represent how strongly two nodes are connected
    • Strongly-connected nodes are pulled closer together than weakly-connected nodes.
    • Which incidentally helps strongly-connected things group together visually

So force-directed graphs provide a convenient way to turn human-unreadable data like this:

The cast of "Les MisÃ©rables" and all of their encounters with one another, in JSON form

Into something much more attractive and meaningful:

Force directed graph in D3.js; Click to try interactive version.

The rows and rows of headachy data are out of sight, and the information overload has completely evaporated. Visualized as a force-directed graph, patterns emerge intuitively with minimal cognitive overhead. The story that the data wants to tell has started to come into focus.

If we stopped here, we'd be able to determine a few high-level things about "Les MisÃ©rables" at a glance. If you are familiar with the story (I'm not), you might even be able to guess which characters are represented by some of the nodes. Not so much with the JSON data.

But more than that, this sort of visualization enables lots of cool possibilities for additional interaction. This particular demo doesn't let you do much-- You can't even click on nodes to see which character they represent, even though that data is present-- but I bet in 30 seconds, you can think of at least 5 features that you could add to play with the data here.

There are TONS of examples.

What if we had the tools to not just show the current state of the system, but to replay how it has changed over time?

It's likely that you'd also want to be able to filter and group nodes by a variety of criteria.

And wouldn't it be handy to arbitrarily add nodes with one click or click and drag to select them?

I don't know exactly why those features would be desirable, but I bet somebody smarter than me does. These tools and others like them can all be developed to reduce the cognitive load on those expensive PhD. brains, allowing them to focus on the problems that they want to solve. Force-directed graphs are just one place to start, and I think they look pretty good.

Other cool ways to interact with data:

with Javascript!

  • Circle packing works really well for the zoom-in-zoom-out nature of complex, hierarchical data, and represents groupings very clearly.

Coding

Overview

I want to lay out a few assumptions that would influence the tools and libraries that I would think of to build the tools described above. Here are a few of them:

  • Javascript is the preferred language
  • The software should run in any modern browser (Chrome, Firefox...)
  • The software needs to interface with a Python backend
  • It will be used by a relatively small number of people
    • i.e. It doesn't need to scale like Instagram
  • It does more than just visualize data; it can also be used to edit and configure.

So with that in mind, here are a few thoughts on specific technologies that would be fun to use in creating this toolkit.

Rad Javascript Libraries

Modern Javascript is incredibly powerful, and developing impressive software is easier than ever with heaps of open-source libraries to choose from. Here are a few that I think are relevant to this purpose.

D3.js

"D3" stands for "Data-Driven Documents." This is a powerful and versatile library that specializes at dynamically rendering data (usually in JSON form) on-screen according to the rules of the client-side script. That means that you can load data into plain text, and the code arranges it dynamically in the browser. It's pretty efficient.

D3 is cool because it already supports alot of data-to-graphic functions, and there is lots of sample code to help with rapid prototyping. All of the data visualization examples above were produced with D3.

D3 doesn't particularly excel at data manipulation, but it's not inconceivable to extend the library's functionality to support that.

To see more examples of what D3 can do, check out d3js.org, and don't miss out on the examples page.
